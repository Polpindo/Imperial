DYNAMIC STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS DUE TO WIND LOADING AND THE IMPACT OF URBAN MICROCLIMATOLOGY—A CASE STUDY OF MH. THAMRIN ROAD, JAKARTA

(An Investigation from Fundamental Physics and Engineering Methodology to Application in the Analysis and Design of Supertall Buildings)

SIGIT N. MASHARY, ST
Senior Structural Engineer at PT. Imperial Tata Konstruksi, Indonesia. Holds a degree in Civil and Structural Engineering from Gadjah Mada University, awarded in 1998.

Abstract

This paper presents a critical evaluation of wind-induced dynamic response for a slender, supertall building, quantifying the impact of site-specific urban microclimate effects against standard code-based analysis. A parametric model of a building with a B/D ratio of 1.9 was analyzed for three heights (H = 348.0, 396.0, and 431.2 m) under three distinct analytical approaches: (1) a standard design wind speed (VSNI = 25.8 m/s) per SNI 1727:2020, (2) an aeroelastic stability check (Scruton Number) based on Eurocode 1 (EN 1991-1-4), and (3) a site-specific corrected design wind speed (Vdesign,corrected = 34.83 m/s) derived from local data indicating a 35% wind amplification due to the urban canyon effect (Santoso et al., 2022).

The SNI-based analysis suggested that the 348.0-m configuration was acceptable (Vcrit = 40.25 m/s safely exceeding VSNI). However, the Eurocode analysis revealed a critical stability concern for all models (Scruton Number ≈ 9.77, falling within the critical range of 8-12), indicating an inherent lack of damping-mass robustness. Compounding this, the microclimate-corrected analysis confirms that while the 348.0-m configuration remains acceptable, the taller H2 and H3 models now fail the stability check, as their Vcrit values (33.95 m/s and 30.45 m/s, respectively) are surpassed by the more realistic design wind speed (34.83 m/s). This dramatic shift in results for the taller, more flexible models demonstrates that relying solely on generalized code-based wind speeds can be critically unconservative. The study concludes that site-specific microclimate analysis, validated against robust international standards, is mandatory to prevent non-conservative design and to accurately assess the need for systemic stiffening or auxiliary damping systems to ensure serviceability and stability.

Keywords: Dynamic Wind Analysis; Supertall Building; Urban Microclimate; Vortex-Induced Vibration (VIV); Aeroelasticity; Wind Code; Jakarta.

1. INTRODUCTION

The development of supertall buildings in dense urban areas such as Jakarta demands an accurate analysis of wind loads. Slender structures are highly susceptible to dynamic wind loads, particularly in tropical regions with complex urban topography. National (SNI) and international (Eurocode, ASCE) design standards provide a basis for loading. However, this study investigates and demonstrates that sole reliance on macro-scale wind data from these standards can create a false perception of safety. This analysis emphasizes the importance of site-specific microclimatology studies to achieve a reliable and safe design.

2. PHYSICO-MATHEMATICAL FORMULATION AND PHENOMENOLOGY OF WIND-STRUCTURE INTERACTION

To profoundly understand the dynamic response of structures, we must establish a rigorous mathematical and phenomenological foundation. This chapter systematically dissects wind-structure interaction, beginning with fundamental physical formulations, followed by a description of the resulting response phenomena, the external factors that modify them, empirical analysis procedures based on regulations, and concluding with their implications for modern engineering analysis approaches.

2.1 Physico-Mathematical Foundations

2.1.1 The Atmospheric Wind Field as a Stochastic Vector Field

The mathematical description of fluid motion begins with the principles of conservation of mass and momentum. For airflow at speeds below Mach 0.3, the incompressibility assumption is highly valid. Thus, the fluid velocity field \(\mathbf{u}(x,t)\) and pressure field \(p(x,t)\) are governed by the Navier-Stokes Equations (Eq. 1 and 2):

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{\rho_a}\nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} \quad \text{(Eq. 1)} \] \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 \quad \text{(Eq. 2)} \]

The chaotic nature of turbulence makes a statistical approach via Reynolds decomposition (Eq. 3) essential:

\[ \mathbf{u}(t) = \overline{\mathbf{U}} + \mathbf{u}'(t) \quad \text{(Eq. 3)} \]

The fluctuating component \(\mathbf{u}'(t)\) is characterized by its Power Spectral Density (PSD), which is related to the autocovariance function through the Wiener-Khinchin Theorem (Eq. 4):

\[ S_{uu}(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} R_{uu}(\tau) e^{-i2\pi f\tau} d\tau \quad \text{(Eq. 4)} \]
2.1.2 Structural Dynamics and Natural Frequency

The response of a structure is modeled as a superposition of its orthogonal vibration modes. Using the Lagrangian formalism, we obtain the modal equation of motion (Eq. 5):

\[ M_i \ddot{q}_i(t) + C_i \dot{q}_i(t) + K_i q_i(t) = F_{aero,i}(t) \quad \text{(Eq. 5)} \]

The most fundamental property of this system is its natural frequency (\(f_n\)), which is the frequency of vibration if the system is allowed to vibrate freely without damping and without external forces (\(C_i=0, F_{aero,i}=0\)). The equation of motion becomes \(M_i \ddot{q}_i + K_i q_i = 0\). The solution to this homogeneous differential equation yields the natural angular frequency \(\omega_n = \sqrt{K_i/M_i}\). The cyclic frequency, commonly used, is:

\[ f_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{K_i}{M_i}} \quad \text{(Eq. 6)} \]

The natural frequency is an intrinsic property determined purely by the stiffness (K) and mass (M) of the structure. This value is crucial as it determines the structure's susceptibility to resonant excitation.

2.1.3 Coupled System Analysis and Aeroelastic Instability

The coupling between the fluid and the structure lies in the force term \(F_{aero,i}(t)\). To analyze instability, the aerodynamic force is linearized, resulting in a homogeneous equation of motion (Eq. 7):

\[ ([M] + [M_{aero}])\ddot{\mathbf{q}} + ([C] + [C_{aero}])\dot{\mathbf{q}} + ([K] + [K_{aero}])\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{(Eq. 7)} \]

Instability occurs when the real part of one of the system's eigenvalues becomes positive (Hopf bifurcation).

2.2 Response Phenomena and Aeroelastic Instability

The response of a structure to wind can be classified into two major categories: forced vibration and self-excited vibration or instability. The most fundamental response is forced vibration, arising from direct interaction with wind turbulence. In the along-wind direction, this response is known as buffeting, where the structure vibrates "back-and-forth" due to random velocity fluctuations acting upon it. In engineering practice, this dynamic effect is often accounted for through the Gust Response Factor (GRF). A more complex, and often more critical, phenomenon for tall buildings is vortex shedding. As wind flows past a building's bluff body profile, it separates from its sides, creating a regular, alternating pattern of vortices known as the von Kármán vortex street. This phenomenon produces a periodic force acting perpendicular (transverse) to the wind direction. Danger arises when the frequency of this vortex shedding approaches one of the structure's natural frequencies (\(f_n\)), causing resonance that significantly amplifies the vibration. Under these conditions, the structure's vibration can "lock-in" the vortex shedding frequency, making this transverse response often the primary determinant for serviceability issues, such as occupant comfort. The Critical Velocity (\(V_{crit}\)) is a key parameter marking the onset of this resonance.

Fundamentally different from forced vibration, aeroelastic instability is a far more dangerous class of phenomena. In these cases, the structure's own motion creates unstable aerodynamic forces, which in turn amplify the motion in a positive feedback loop. A prime example is galloping, a large-amplitude, across-wind oscillation that can occur on non-circular cross-sections, effectively acting as negative aerodynamic damping. The most catastrophic form of instability is flutter, involving the dynamic coupling of bending and torsional vibration modes, where wind energy is absorbed by the system, causing amplitudes to grow explosively until failure. Its static counterpart is divergence, where the aerodynamic twisting moment grows faster than the internal elastic restoring moment, leading to continuous twisting until failure.

2.3 External Modifying Factors: Influence of the Surrounding Environment

2.3.1 Aerodynamic Interference and Urban Microclimatology Effects

The characteristics of the wind arriving at a building's face are determined not only by the macro-climate but are also significantly modified by the immediate local environment. In dense urban settings, the phenomenon of aerodynamic interference becomes highly dominant. The presence of surrounding buildings can drastically alter flow patterns, both through the channeling effect, where wind is accelerated as it flows through narrow gaps between buildings, and through wake buffeting, where a downstream building is exposed to the highly turbulent flow from an upstream one.

This phenomenon is no longer merely theoretical but has been specifically quantified for the Indonesian urban context. A key study by Santoso et al. (2022), using a combination of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations and field validation, specifically investigated the urban canyon effect in Jakarta's financial district. These findings were corroborated by a technical cooperation report between BMKG and the Ministry of Public Works and Housing (PUPR) (2021), which also analyzed micro-scale wind patterns in the Thamrin-Sudirman corridor for high-rise building design. Collectively, these studies provide strong evidence that local wind speed amplification can reach a factor of 1.35 (or 35%) or more compared to the open-terrain wind speeds assumed by general standards. This fact has profound implications, as it means that an analysis relying solely on standard data becomes highly non-conservative, which is the core of the case study discussed in this paper.

2.3.2 Topographic Effects

In addition to the built environment, natural topography also plays a significant role. The acceleration of wind flow (known as speed-up) occurs when wind passes over hills or steep escarpments due to the compression of streamlines. This effect is explicitly quantified in design standards through the Topography Factor (\(K_{zt}\)) and must be accounted for to improve the accuracy of the design wind speed.

2.4 Design Code-Based Analysis Approach (Empirical)

Design standards such as SNI 1727:2020, ASCE 7-22, and Eurocode 1 (EN 1991-1-4) provide empirical procedures to estimate wind loads and structural responses. These procedures simplify complex physics into a series of manageable calculation steps.

2.4.1 Main Wind Force Resisting System (Along-Wind) Procedure per SNI/ASCE
  1. Basic Wind Speed (V): Determined from wind maps for a specific return period and building risk category (SNI 1727:2020, Chapter 26).
  2. Velocity Pressure (\(q_z\)): Wind speed is converted into pressure that varies with height \(z\). \[ q_z = 0.613 K_z K_{zt} K_d V^2 \quad \text{(SNI 1727:2020, Eq. 26.10-1)} \]
  3. Design Wind Pressure (p): The final pressure is calculated by applying the GRF (\(G\)) and external pressure coefficients (\(C_p\)). \[ p = q_z G C_p \quad \text{(SNI 1727:2020, §27.3.1)} \]
2.4.2 Simplified VIV Screening Procedure (SNI/ASCE)
  1. Analysis Trigger: Generally required for tall and slender buildings (e.g., \(H > 120\) m and \(H/B > 4\) according to ASCE 7-22, §26.12.1).
  2. Calculation of Critical Velocity (\(V_{crit}\)): The wind speed at which the vortex shedding frequency equals the structure's natural frequency is calculated as: \[ V_{crit} = \frac{f_n D}{St} \quad \text{(Eq. 9)} \]
  3. Critical Evaluation: If \(V_{crit}\) is less than the relevant design wind speed, VIV is a potentially serious issue, and further analysis is mandatory.
2.4.3 VIV Stability Evaluation Procedure per Eurocode 1

Eurocode 1 (EN 1991-1-4, Annex E) provides a more physically oriented methodology by evaluating the structure's inherent capacity to resist vibrations through non-dimensional parameters.

  1. Structural Damping: Eurocode uses the logarithmic decrement of damping (\(\delta_s\)), which relates to the critical damping ratio (\(\zeta\)) by: \[ \delta_s = 2\pi\zeta \quad \text{(Eq. 10)} \]
  2. Stability Parameter (Scruton Number - Sc): This parameter measures the ratio of the structure's damping-mass capacity to the aerodynamic excitation forces. It is the primary indicator of VIV stability. \[ Sc = \frac{2 m_{e,len} \delta_s}{\rho_a D^2} \quad \text{(Eq. 11)} \] where \(m_{e,len}\) is the effective mass per unit length. A high \(Sc\) value indicates a more inherently stable structure.
  3. Stability Criterion: The core of the Eurocode method is comparing the structure's actual Scruton Number (\(Sc\)) with the required minimum value (\(Sc_{min}\)). \[ Sc > Sc_{min} \] If this condition is met, large-amplitude VIV is not expected to occur. The value of \(Sc_{min}\) itself is determined from graphs or formulas in the standard (e.g., EN 1991-1-4, Figure E.2) and typically has a value around 8-12 for rectangular sections. If this criterion is not met, a detailed VIV analysis or mitigation measures become mandatory.

2.5 Implications and Hierarchy of Analysis Approaches

Understanding this spectrum of phenomena leads to a hierarchy of analysis approaches, from empirical code-based methods to more sophisticated full-scale simulations:

  • Codes and Standards: The first step for preliminary design and screening. Sufficient for regular and stiff buildings, but may not cover all complex scenarios, especially regarding interference and topographic effects, as demonstrated in this case study.
  • Numerical Simulation (CFD): Computational Fluid Dynamics allows for detailed flow visualization and, when coupled with a structural model (FEA) in a Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) simulation, can directly predict aeroelastic responses. Useful for parametric studies and investigating local effects.
  • Wind Tunnel Testing: This remains the "gold standard" in wind engineering, especially for critical, tall, and irregular projects. Techniques like High-Frequency Force Balance (HFFB) and aeroelastic model testing provide the most reliable data for dynamic response.

It is important to note that CFD and wind tunnel testing represent advanced studies that provide the highest precision but are beyond the scope of the analysis performed in this paper. This paper specifically uses the first approach: a parametric analysis based on Design Standard procedures. This approach is then extended by incorporating a microclimatology correction factor to test and demonstrate the limitations of the standard method. It must be emphasized that this correction factor itself is derived from the findings of Santoso et al., which were based on CFD analysis. The data from that advanced study is not replicated but rather applied in this paper through an approach of informed extrapolation to create a more realistic loading scenario. The detailed procedure for this extrapolation is described in APPENDIX 5.

3. ANALYSIS METHODOLOGY

The research approach in this study is a tri-comparative parametric analysis designed to critically evaluate how design conclusions can drastically change depending on the standard used and the inclusion of local microclimatology data. This methodology specifically compares the results obtained from three different analytical branches: (1) the simple screening procedure according to SNI 1727:2020; (2) the more detailed VIV analysis procedure from Eurocode 1; and (3) a modified SNI analysis incorporating site-specific data from the CFD research by Santoso et al.

3.1 Research Design Framework

This study adopts a comparative design with three parallel analysis branches applied to the same set of structural models:

  1. SNI Analysis Branch (Domestic Baseline): Implements a direct comparison between the design wind speed from SNI 1727:2020 and the structure's critical velocity. This branch represents the most fundamental screening approach.
  2. Eurocode 1 Analysis Branch (International Benchmark): Applies the more comprehensive VIV evaluation procedure from Annex E of EN 1991-1-4, involving the aerodynamic stability parameter (Scruton Number). This serves as a benchmark for more advanced engineering practice.
  3. Corrected SNI Analysis Branch (Local Reality Case Study): Repeats the SNI analysis, but with a wind speed input that has been calibrated using the amplification factor from the site-specific CFD study for the Jl. MH. Thamrin corridor in Jakarta.

5. DESIGN RECOMMENDATIONS AND MITIGATION STRATEGIES

The findings from Chapter 4 unequivocally demonstrate that the analyzed design model has fundamental weaknesses, both from the perspective of inherent stability (as identified by Eurocode) and performance under realistic local wind conditions (as shown by the Corrected SNI analysis). Thus, proceeding with the design without significant engineering intervention is unacceptable. This chapter outlines a hierarchy of recommended mitigation strategies to address the identified design failures.

5.1 Hierarchy of Design Interventions

  1. Primary Strategy: Systemic Stiffness Enhancement to raise the Critical Velocity (\(V_{crit}\)).
  2. Secondary Strategy: Implementation of a Tuned Mass Damper (TMD) to increase damping and control vibrational response.

5.2 Primary Strategy: Systemic Stiffness Enhancement

The fundamental goal of this strategy is to directly address the root problem identified in the SNI analysis: ensuring that the structure's Critical Velocity (\(V_{crit}\)) is significantly higher than the realistic design wind speed (\(V_{design,corrected} = 34.83\) m/s). Referring to Eq. 9 (\(V_{crit} = f_n D / St\)), the most effective way to raise \(V_{crit}\) is to increase the structure's fundamental natural frequency (\(f_n\)). According to Eq. 6 (\(f_n \propto \sqrt{K/M}\)), this can be achieved by increasing the lateral stiffness (K) of the main force-resisting system.

Alternative structural systems that are inherently much stiffer and suitable for supertall buildings include:

  • Reinforced RC Core Wall System: While the basis of many designs, the central core can be enlarged or its shape optimized (e.g., with flanges) to significantly increase stiffness.
  • Outrigger and Belt Truss System: This is one of the most efficient stiffness-enhancing strategies. Rigid outriggers connect the building's central core to the exterior perimeter columns. Thus, the entire width of the building is mobilized to resist the overturning moment, dramatically increasing the effective stiffness and, consequently, raising \(f_n\) and \(V_{crit}\).
  • Tube Systems: This concept utilizes the building's facade as the primary lateral force-resisting structure. Variations include the framed tube, trussed tube, and tube-in-tube, all of which offer very high levels of stiffness.

5.3 Secondary Strategy: Implementation of a Tuned Mass Damper (TMD)

The implementation of a Tuned Mass Damper (TMD) becomes relevant and necessary under a very specific design condition: when the primary structural system has been designed with adequate strength and stiffness to meet the fundamental stability criterion (\(V_{crit} > V_{design}\)), but still exhibits a vibrational response that exceeds occupant comfort limits (peak acceleration). Furthermore, the added damping from a TMD directly increases the overall system's Scruton Number (\(Sc\)), aiding in the fulfillment of stricter stability criteria as required by Eurocode.

  • Effectiveness and Mechanism: A TMD is a secondary mass-spring-damper system that is "tuned" to resonate at the natural frequency of the structure it is intended to damp. When the building begins to vibrate at its frequency, the TMD oscillates out of phase, effectively "absorbing" and dissipating the vibrational energy through its damping element (e.g., viscous dampers).

It is important to note that a TMD does not add static stiffness to the building and does not change the natural frequency (\(f_n\)) or the Critical Velocity (\(V_{crit}\)). Its primary role is to add damping (\(\zeta\)) and reduce the amplitude of vibration once it occurs. Its effectiveness in reducing peak acceleration can be as high as 30-50%, making it a very powerful solution for serviceability issues.

5.4 Synthesis of Design Recommendations

For the design of a supertall building in a location with complex wind conditions like the Jl. MH. Thamrin corridor, the most reliable and comprehensive approach is a hybrid approach:

  1. Optimize the Primary Structural System: Use a stiffness-efficient system such as an outrigger system to ensure fundamental stability is met (\(V_{crit}\) significantly higher than \(V_{design, corrected}\)).
  2. Integrate an Auxiliary Damping System: Install a Tuned Mass Damper (TMD) to ensure occupant comfort criteria (peak acceleration) are met and to enhance inherent stability (increase the Scruton Number).

This combination ensures the building is safe from aeroelastic failure while also being comfortable for occupancy, addressing all weaknesses identified in the analysis in Chapter 4.

APPENDIX 5: METHODOLOGY FOR EXTRAPOLATING SANTOSO ET. AL. WIND PROFILE

A5.1 Objective and Background

This appendix aims to provide methodological transparency regarding how the correction factor and reference wind profile from the research of Santoso et al. are used in this paper. As stated in Chapter 2, their CFD study identified significant wind speed amplification due to the urban canyon effect in the dense urban corridor.

For the primary comparative analysis in Chapter 4, a single average correction factor (\(K_{micro} = 1.35\)) was used to simplify the comparison. However, in physical reality, wind amplification is not uniform along the building's height. CFD studies like that of Santoso et al. show that the maximum wind acceleration (often called the jet effect) tends to occur in the upper-middle part of the "alley" between buildings, not at the very top.

Therefore, this appendix explains the process of informed extrapolation used to derive a more realistic non-uniform wind profile. This profile, while not used in the main tabular calculations, serves as a crucial reference for understanding the true impact of microclimatology and is conceptually validated in the graphical visualizations in Chapter 4.

A5.2 Fundamental Principles from the Source Study

The key principles extracted from the findings of Santoso et al. are as follows:

  1. Peak Amplification: The greatest wind speed amplification occurs not at the top of the buildings but at a height of approximately 60-70% of the surrounding buildings' total height.
  2. Non-Linear Profile: The wind speed profile within an urban canyon does not follow the standard power law profile. It increases, reaches a peak, and then may slightly decrease or slow its rate of increase near the top as the flow "escapes" the confinement of the canyon.

A5.3 Two-Stage Extrapolation Process

The process to convert these principles into a usable loading profile consists of two stages, visualized below:

  1. Stage 1: Abstraction of Principles into a Generic Amplification Profile. The principles above are abstracted into a non-dimensional "Amplification Factor" curve. The vertical axis is the multiplication factor (\(K_{micro}\)), and the horizontal axis is the normalized height (\(z/H\)). This curve qualitatively replicates the shape of the amplification profile found in CFD studies.
  2. Stage 2: Application of the Profile to the Case Study. This generic amplification profile is then applied to the basic wind speed from SNI (\(V_{SNI}\)) for one of the case study models (e.g., H₁ = 348.0 m) to generate a realistic, height-specific corrected wind speed profile.

A5.4 Visualization of the Extrapolation Process

Left (Stage 1 - Principle): This graph shows the generic "Wind Amplification Factor Profile" abstracted from the findings of Santoso et al. The Y-axis is the multiplication factor, and the X-axis is the normalized height (z/H). The peak amplification (around 1.6x) occurs at 60-70% of the total structure height, with an average value approaching 1.35.
Right (Stage 2 - Application): This graph shows the application of the generic profile to the specific model H₁ (348.0 m). The basic SNI wind speed (dashed line, 25.8 m/s) is multiplied by the amplification factor from the left graph at each height point. The result is the "Santoso Corrected Velocity Profile" (solid yellow line), representing a more realistic loading scenario.

A5.5 Justification and Limitations

It is crucial to state that the profile generated from this methodology is not a replication of raw data from the source study but rather a physically informed representation. Its purpose is to demonstrate qualitatively and quantitatively how a more realistic non-uniform wind profile would impact the analysis compared to the assumption of a uniform average amplification. This approach reinforces the main conclusion of the paper that microclimatological effects are not only significant but also complex, further underscoring the need for site-specific analysis.

A6.1 Background and Jakarta Context

Design decisions for skyscrapers in Jakarta are inseparable from three interconnected realities: (1) extremely high property values in the central business district, (2) microclimatological wind conditions amplified by the urban canyon effect, and (3) significant seismic risk according to SNI 1726:2019. This appendix discusses the engineering dilemma between massively stiffening a structure versus using auxiliary damping systems (TMDs) in this specific context.

A6.3 Interconnection of Wind and Seismic Design

The design for wind (a serviceability load) and earthquakes (an ultimate load) follows different philosophies, which creates both conflicts and synergies in structural design in Jakarta.

Design Aspect Wind Design Demands Seismic Design Demands (SNI 1726:2019)
Stiffness vs. Flexibility Requires high stiffness to limit drift and acceleration. Requires ductility (controlled flexibility) to dissipate energy. An overly stiff structure can attract very large seismic forces.
Building Mass Greater mass is slightly beneficial for damping vibrations. Mass is the "enemy." The greater the mass (m), the greater the seismic force (F=ma) that must be resisted.
Role of TMD Highly effective for comfort (damping acceleration). Ineffective as a primary seismic force-resisting system because earthquakes have a broad and random frequency content.

A6.4 Synthesis: Optimal Design Strategy for Jakarta

Considering all factors, the most logical, safe, and cost-effective design strategy for a skyscraper in Jakarta is a layered hybrid approach:

  1. Layer 1: Seismic Strength & Ductility (Design Basis): Design the primary structure as a Dual System per SNI 1726:2019, using a combination of a Reinforced Concrete (RC) Core Wall and a Special Moment Resisting Frame. This is a non-negotiable safety baseline.
  2. Layer 2: Efficient Wind Stiffness: Rather than massively enlarging the core, efficiently increase stiffness with an Outrigger and Belt Truss system. This system controls drift from both wind and seismic loads without excessively increasing weight.
  3. Layer 3: Comfort & Performance Tuning: After strength and drift criteria are met, install a Tuned Mass Damper (TMD). The TMD acts as a "finishing layer" that specifically targets and dampens wind-induced vibrations, ensuring occupant comfort.

With this layered strategy, all objectives are achieved: the building is safe against earthquakes, stiff against wind drift, comfortable for occupants, and efficient both spatially and financially.
